1. The Dataset: How the AI "Senses" a Storm

Imagine training an AI solely on Atlantic Hurricanes. If you test it on Pacific Typhoons, it fails. This is because different oceans (Basins) have different steering wind patterns. Here is what our AI feeds on, utilizing the TropiCycloneNet Dataset (TCND).

Data_3d

ERA5 Gridded Patches

Think of this as 13-channel satellite imagery. But instead of RGB colors, the AI sees Sea Surface Temperature (SST), and 3D wind slices (U/V) and pressure heights (Z) from low above the ocean up to 200 hPa high in the atmosphere.

Extracted via SpatialEncoder.

Data_1d

Tabular Tracking Data

The "vital signs" of the storm from the IBTrACS database. It tracks exactly where the storm currently is (Longitude, Latitude), its central Pressure, and current Wind speeds.

Extracted via TrackEncoder.

Env-Data

77D Environment Profile

Context matters. This is a massive 77-feature array telling the AI the month of the year, the exact ocean area (one-hot encoded), historical intensity changes, and local velocity vectors.

Extracted via EnvEncoder.

2. Architecture Flow: The Code Execution Path

How does train.py and backbone.py process this data to predict intensity and direction? Hover over the blocks to see exactly what happens under the hood.

Data_3d & Data_1d
Raw Tensors: 3D NetCDF grid arrays (13, 81, 81) and 1D basic tracking features normalized to floats.
PhysicsExtractor (dataset.py)
Mathematical calculation of 8 raw physical anchors: SST anomaly, wind shear magnitude, Coriolis force, and proxies for Maximum Potential Intensity.
Env-Data (dataset.py)
Parsing the 77-dim vectors describing the specific regional context like 'steering winds' and 'basin location'.
PhysicsEncoder
Transforms 8D physics numbers into z_phys: An invariant sub-space representing pure thermodynamics.
EnvEncoder
Transforms 77D arrays into z_env: A synoptic sub-space representing local wind steering. This is allowed to shift between oceans.
TaskHeads (Intensity & Direction)
z_phys and z_env are concatenated. A Multi-Layer Perceptron predicts 5 Intensity classes (including 'Rapid Intensification') and 4 Direction classes.

3. The Algorithms: Domain Generalization

We train on 5 ocean basins and test completely "zero-shot" on the 6th (Leave-One-Basin-Out strategy). Click to expand the DG (Domain Generalization) methods defined in dg_methods.py.

Baseline ERM (Empirical Risk Minimization)
▼

The standard way to do deep learning. You dump data from all 5 source basins into one big bucket, shuffle, and minimize the average loss using Cross-Entropy. Unsurprisingly, ERM often learns "cheats" specific to the source oceans and fails on the 6th.

Classic DG IRM (Invariant Risk Minimization)
▼

A brilliant invention from Arjovsky et al. (2019). It creates a dummy classifier $w=1$ applied to the features. It penalizes the gradients of the loss with respect to $w$ across different environments (basins).

Translation: The AI is heavily penalized if its reasoning strategy varies heavily from ocean to ocean. The logic must be consistently applicable everywhere.

Novel Method PhysIRM: Physics-Guided IRM (Our Code)
▼

Standard IRM forces the entire model to be invariant. But in weather, environmental steering winds *do* legitimately change across oceans! Forcing them to be invariant hurts the model.

The PhysIRM Logic: We split the brain. Let z_env learn the shifting regional patterns. But apply the strict IRM penalty only on z_phys. We force the thermodynamic equations (like Carnot Heat Engine principles: SST, evaporation, Coriolis) to remain perfectly invariant across all oceans.

4. Code Truths: Approximations & Limitations

Physics is infinitely complex. In making it computable, we had to adopt physical approximations and deviations in the codebase (specifically in dataset.py).

Approximation

The Coriolis Parameter Shortcut

The Coriolis force relies on the exact latitude of a storm (2 * Omega * sin(lat)). To enforce broad basin-level grouping, the codebase uses a basin-average latitude.

For example, in the code:
WP: 3.14 (avg lat ~13°N)
SI: -3.86 (avg lat ~-17°S)

Deviation: Individual storm trajectories that stray far from the basin mean will have slightly warped physical anchors.

Approximation

Atmospheric Proxies

Full 3D calculus is expensive. The code calculates proxies:

  • Outflow Proxy: Approximated simply as the normalized mean Z (Geopotential Height) at the 200 hPa level.
  • Steering Proxy: Approximated as the mean Z at the 500 hPa level.
Limitation

Out-of-Support Synoptic Environments

While PhysIRM perfectly anchors thermodynamic physics, the z_env branch isn't magical. If we test on a target basin that has a purely unprecedented synoptic wind layout—one entirely unrepresented in the 5 source basins—the EnvEncoder has no historical reference for interpolation. The zero-shot transfer will struggle because the environment falls outside the training distribution support.

5. Engineering & Systems: Hardware Awareness

Training complex physics-informed models requires efficient system-level optimizations. Recent updates to the codebase runframes (train.py and dataset.py) ensure maximum hardware scalability.

train.py

Intelligent Device Auto-Selection

The training script now automatically detects the optimal hardware backend for execution without manual intervention. It scales seamlessly across platforms:

  • CUDA: Priority detection for NVIDIA GPUs.
  • MPS: Native support for Apple Silicon (M-series Macs) for accelerated local testing.
  • CPU: Fallback mode for universal compatibility.
dataset.py

Dynamic Memory Pinning

Loading 3D NetCDF climate data can bottleneck the pipeline. The DataLoader now intelligently enables pin_memory=True only when a CUDA-enabled GPU is physically available.

Optimization: This prevents severe pageable memory overheads and crashes when users run the pipeline locally on Apple Silicon (MPS) or standard CPUs.
